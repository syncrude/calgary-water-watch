TL;DR: What Residents Need to Know

  • The 2027 "Pivot": While rates are rising by ~6.5% now [cite: 126, 127], residents should prepare for a projected 14% rate increase in 2027 to fund massive infrastructure repairs[cite: 192].
  • No-Tax Funding: Your property taxes do not pay for water[cite: 170]. The utility is "no-net-tax," meaning every dollar for repairs comes directly from your monthly bill[cite: 169, 171].
  • Infrastructure Debt: To fix the failing feeder main network, the city is borrowing $1.2 billion, which will push the utility’s debt capacity to 50% by 2027[cite: 159, 167].
  • The Efficiency Paradox: As Calgarians conserve more water, the price per liter often goes up because the city's fixed costs (pipes, labor) stay the same regardless of usage[cite: 178, 183].

1. Potable Production vs. Delivery

The city operates two major plants—Bearspaw and Glenmore—with a combined capacity of 950 megalitres per day[cite: 120]. While the water itself is high quality, the cost of "cleaning" it is highly sensitive to spring runoff and turbidity levels[cite: 123].

2. The Sanitary Loop: Wastewater Economics

It is significantly more expensive to clean water after use than to purify it for drinking[cite: 132]. The 2025 residential wastewater rate of $1.7959 per 1,000L is roughly 12% higher than the potable supply rate[cite: 134].

The Return Factor Formula

Calgary assumes that 90% of the water you use returns to the sewer[cite: 136]. Your bill is calculated as:

$$C_{ww} = (V_{metered} \times 0.90) \times R_{usage}$$

Where $C_{ww}$ is the charge and $V_{metered}$ is your total consumption[cite: 137, 138, 139].

Industrial Surcharges

Businesses that dump "extra-strength" waste (oil, grease, or chemicals) pay a premium to cover the intensified aeration and chemical costs at the Bonnybrook plant[cite: 141, 154].

$$R = 0.1443B + 0.1147S + 0.1947G$$

Calculation based on Biological Oxygen Demand (B), Suspended Solids (S), and Grease (G)[cite: 145, 146, 147].

3. Infrastructure & The "Resilience Premium"

Following the Bearspaw feeder main failures, the cost of maintaining the 5,350km pipe network has shifted from maintenance to emergency replacement[cite: 125, 157]. The capital budget for the water utility is seeing a near five-fold increase between 2024 and 2026[cite: 164].

The Infrastructure Deficit

Calgary’s water network consists of over 5,000km of pipes. The recent failures in the feeder main system have highlighted a critical vulnerability: the aging "Prestressed Concrete Cylinder Pipes" (PCCP). Replacing and reinforcing these is the primary driver behind the projected debt increases.

Visualizing the scale: These pipes are large enough for a person to stand inside and are reinforced with miles of high-tension steel wire.

The Cost of "Non-Revenue" Water

When water is lost to leaks in the distribution system, it is known as "non-revenue water." Not only does the city lose the $0.8094 per cubic meter spent on treatment chemicals and energy, but the "lost" water represents a direct hit to the utility's ability to fund future repairs without raising rates further.
